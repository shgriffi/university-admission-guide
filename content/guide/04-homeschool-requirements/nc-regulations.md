# North Carolina Homeschool Regulations

**For**: Homeschool Students (Class of [YEAR])
**Last Updated**: February 14, 2026

---

## Introduction

Understanding North Carolina homeschool law is essential for your family's compliance and for your college applications. While universities do NOT require you to submit proof of NC homeschool compliance, you must be legally compliant with state regulations to operate as a homeschool.

This guide summarizes NC homeschool law, registration requirements, ongoing obligations, and resources. It's written specifically for your family to ensure compliance and understand what documentation universities may reference (even if they don't require submission).

**IMPORTANT**: This guide is based on NC General Statutes as of 2026. State laws can change. Always verify current requirements with the NC Division of Non-Public Education (DNPE) and consult official sources.

---

## NC Homeschool Law Overview

### Legal Basis

**North Carolina General Statute 115C, Article 39**:
- Homeschools in NC are classified as "non-public schools"
- Parents have the legal right to homeschool their children
- Homeschools must comply with specific state requirements
- NC Division of Non-Public Education (DNPE) oversees homeschool compliance

**Key Principle**: Homeschooling is legal in North Carolina. Parents who comply with state law have full authority to educate their children at home.

### Three Homeschool Options in NC

North Carolina offers three legal structures for homeschooling:

#### Option 1: Home School (Most Common)

**Definition**: Non-public school operated by parent(s) for their own children

**Requirements**:
- File Notice of Intent with NC DNPE
- Administer annual standardized test OR alternative assessment
- Maintain attendance records (9 calendar months per year)
- Operate school with at least one parent/guardian who has high school diploma or GED
- Teach basic education subjects (reading, writing, math, science, social studies)

**Oversight**: NC Division of Non-Public Education (DNPE)

**Pros**:
- Full parental control over curriculum and schedule
- Minimal state oversight
- Flexibility in teaching methods

**Cons**:
- Parent must handle all administrative tasks (filing, testing, records)
- No external accreditation unless you seek it separately

#### Option 2: Private Church School or Qualified Non-Public School

**Definition**: Homeschool operated under umbrella of church or qualified non-public school

**Requirements**:
- School (church or private school) files Notice of Intent with DNPE
- School maintains records and ensures compliance
- Students follow school's curriculum and requirements
- May or may not require annual testing (depends on school)

**Oversight**: Church or private school (which is registered with DNPE)

**Pros**:
- School handles administrative tasks
- May provide transcript and diploma services
- Some schools offer accreditation

**Cons**:
- Less flexibility (must follow school's requirements)
- May have enrollment fees or tuition
- Curriculum may be prescribed by school

#### Option 3: Private Tutor

**Definition**: Child receives instruction from qualified tutor instead of parent

**Requirements**:
- Tutor must have bachelor's degree
- Tutor must be certified to teach in subject area
- Parent files Notice of Intent (or tutor does)

**Oversight**: DNPE

**Pros**:
- Professional instruction
- Can hire specialists for different subjects

**Cons**:
- Expensive (hiring certified teachers)
- Less parental involvement
- Still requires compliance with NC regulations

### Which Option is Most Common?

**Option 1 (Home School)** is the most common choice for NC homeschool families. This guide assumes your family is using Option 1 unless you specify otherwise.

## Registration Requirements

### Notice of Intent to Operate a Home School

**What It Is**: Official registration of your homeschool with the state

**When to File**:
- Before beginning homeschool for the first time
- Within 30 days of establishing residency in NC (if moving from another state)
- Annually (some sources indicate annual filing, others indicate one-time registration - verify current requirement)

**Where to File**:
- NC Division of Non-Public Education (DNPE)
- Online through DNPE website: https://www.ncdnpe.org/
- Or by mail to DNPE office

**What Information is Required**:
- Name of school (e.g., "[Your Homeschool Name]")
- School address (typically your home address)
- Name of owner/chief administrator (parent's name)
- Name(s) of student(s)
- Grade level(s) of student(s)
- Parent/guardian signature certifying compliance with NC law

**Processing**:
- DNPE reviews and approves Notice of Intent
- You receive confirmation (keep this on file)
- Your homeschool is now legally registered in NC

**Fee**:
- No fee to register as homeschool in NC (free)

**Renewal**:
- Verify whether NC requires annual renewal of Notice of Intent or if it's one-time registration
- Check DNPE website for current requirements

### School Name Requirements

**Choosing a School Name**:
- Can use family name + "Homeschool" (e.g., "[Your Homeschool Name]")
- Can use family name + "Academy" (e.g., "[Your School Name]")
- Can create unique name (e.g., "Raleigh Home Learning Center")

**Restrictions**:
- Cannot imply public school status
- Cannot use misleading names suggesting accreditation you don't have
- Must be distinguishable from existing NC schools

**Best Practice**: Simple family name + "Homeschool" or "Academy" is standard and accepted.

## Ongoing Annual Requirements

### 1. Annual Standardized Testing or Assessment

**Requirement**: Students must demonstrate academic progress through annual standardized testing OR alternative assessment

**Acceptable Testing Options**:
- **Nationally Standardized Tests** (at grade level):
  - Iowa Tests of Basic Skills (ITBS)
  - Stanford Achievement Test
  - California Achievement Test (CAT)
  - TerraNova
  - Other nationally normed standardized tests

**Who Administers the Test**:
- Parent can administer test at home (for most tests)
- Or use qualified test administrator (some families hire testing services)
- No requirement for certified teacher to administer (in most cases)

**When to Test**:
- Annually (once per school year)
- Typically in spring (March-May), but can be anytime during school year
- Must test in grades required by NC law (verify which grades - typically all K-12)

**Minimum Score Requirements**:
- NC does NOT require minimum passing scores on standardized tests
- Tests are to demonstrate progress, not to meet specific benchmarks
- Low scores do NOT result in penalties (in most cases)

**What If Student Scores Low**:
- No automatic intervention by state
- Parent should assess whether additional support is needed
- Continue homeschooling with adjustments as needed

**Reporting Requirements**:
- Test scores do NOT need to be submitted to state
- Parent keeps test scores on file for their records
- May be requested if DNPE conducts compliance review (rare)

**Cost**:
- Varies by test and provider
- Typically $50-150 per student per year
- Some testing services offer group discounts or homeschool rates

**Alternative Assessment**:
- Some NC homeschool options allow alternative assessment instead of standardized testing
- Verify current NC law on whether portfolio assessment or other alternatives are acceptable
- Most families use standardized testing (simplest option)

### 2. Attendance Records

**Requirement**: Homeschool must operate for at least nine calendar months per year

**What "Nine Months" Means**:
- School year must cover nine consecutive calendar months
- Does NOT mean nine months of daily instruction (flexible scheduling allowed)
- Does NOT specify number of days or hours per month

**Typical School Year**:
- August/September through May/June (nine months)
- Can start earlier or later as long as nine consecutive months are covered

**Record Keeping**:
- Parent maintains attendance records showing school was in operation for nine months
- Can be simple log or calendar marking school days
- No specific format required by state

**Verification**:
- Attendance records kept by parent (not submitted to state)
- May be requested if DNPE conducts compliance review

**What Counts as School Days**:
- Traditional academic instruction at home
- Field trips and educational outings
- Co-op classes or group learning
- Online courses
- Any educational activities as determined by parent

### 3. Immunization Records

**Requirement**: Students must meet NC immunization requirements (same as public school students)

**Required Immunizations** (verify current NC schedule):
- Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis (DTaP)
- Polio
- Measles, Mumps, Rubella (MMR)
- Hepatitis B
- Varicella (Chickenpox)
- Others as specified by NC Department of Health

**Exemptions Available**:
- **Medical Exemption**: For students with medical contraindications (requires physician statement)
- **Religious Exemption**: For families with genuine religious objection to immunization (requires written statement)

**How to Document**:
- Keep immunization records from healthcare provider
- Or exemption forms if claiming exemption
- Not submitted to DNPE but must be maintained

**College Consideration**:
- Most universities require immunization records for enrollment
- If claiming exemption for homeschool, verify whether college will allow exemptions
- Plan ahead if immunizations will be needed for college

### 4. Curriculum and Instruction

**Required Subjects**: NC homeschools must provide instruction in:
- Reading
- Writing
- Mathematics
- Science
- Social Studies (including history and government)

**No Specific Curriculum Required**:
- Parents choose curriculum and materials
- No state approval or pre-approval needed
- Can use published curriculum, create your own, or mix approaches

**No Required Courses for High School**:
- NC does not specify required high school courses (e.g., "must take 4 years of English")
- Parent determines appropriate coursework based on student's needs and college goals
- College admissions requirements (not state law) dictate what courses to include

**Teaching Hours**:
- No specific daily or weekly hour requirements in NC
- Parents determine appropriate instructional time

### 5. Teacher Qualifications (Parent)

**Minimum Requirement**: At least one parent/guardian must have high school diploma or equivalent (GED)

**No Teaching Certificate Required**: Parents do NOT need:
- College degree
- Teaching certification
- Formal training in education

**Outside Instructors**:
- Parents can hire tutors or instructors for specific subjects
- Tutors do not need to be certified teachers
- Online courses and co-op classes are allowed

### 6. Annual Reporting to State

**Current NC Law** (verify for updates):
- Homeschools file Notice of Intent initially (see Registration Requirements above)
- Most sources indicate NO annual reporting beyond maintaining records
- Some homeschool structures may require annual renewal of Notice of Intent

**What You Do NOT Report to State**:
- Test scores (kept by parent, not submitted)
- Curriculum details
- Grades or transcripts
- Course descriptions

**What You DO Keep on File** (in case of review):
- Attendance records (showing nine months operation)
- Standardized test results
- Immunization records or exemptions
- Curriculum materials or documentation

## High School Requirements for College Preparation

### NC Does Not Specify High School Graduation Requirements for Homeschools

**What This Means**:
- No state-mandated credit requirements for homeschool graduation
- No required courses (unlike public schools which require specific courses)
- No minimum GPA or testing for homeschool diploma

**Parent Determines Graduation Requirements**:
- You decide how many credits are needed
- You decide what courses constitute a diploma
- You issue diploma when student meets your requirements

### Recommended High School Coursework for College

While NC doesn't require specific courses, **colleges expect** homeschool applicants to have completed rigorous college-prep curriculum:

**Minimum Recommended** (for competitive colleges):
- **English**: 4 credits (one per year, 9th-12th grade)
- **Mathematics**: 4 credits (Algebra I, Geometry, Algebra II, Pre-Calculus or higher)
- **Science**: 4 credits (Biology, Chemistry, Physics, plus one additional - at least 3 with labs)
- **Social Studies**: 4 credits (World History, US History, Government, Economics, etc.)
- **Foreign Language**: 2-4 credits (same language, consecutive years)
- **Arts**: 1 credit (visual art, music, theater)
- **Electives**: 3-4 credits (additional subjects of interest)
- **Physical Education/Health**: 1 credit

**Total**: 23-28 credits (similar to traditional high school)

**For Engineering/STEM Majors**: Emphasize advanced math (Calculus) and science (AP Chemistry, AP Physics)

**For Business Majors**: Include economics, statistics, advanced math

### Transcript and Diploma

**Parent Issues Transcript and Diploma**:
- You create transcript documenting all coursework and grades (see transcripts.md)
- You issue diploma certifying completion of high school
- Both are accepted by colleges (no state-issued diploma needed)

**Format**:
- See transcripts.md for detailed transcript guidance
- Diploma can be simple certificate stating:
  - Student name
  - "Has successfully completed the requirements for high school graduation"
  - School name (e.g., [Your Homeschool Name])
  - Graduation date
  - Parent signature as school administrator

## Records and Documentation to Maintain

### Required Records (Keep on File)

**Attendance Records**:
- Format: Simple calendar, log, or attendance book
- Content: Dates school was in session (showing nine months per year)
- How Long: At least through high school graduation (recommend keeping permanently)

**Standardized Test Scores**:
- Keep all annual test results
- How Long: Permanently (useful for college applications)

**Immunization Records**:
- Official immunization record from healthcare provider
- Or exemption documentation
- How Long: Permanently (needed for college enrollment)

**Curriculum Documentation** (Recommended, Not Required):
- List of textbooks and materials used
- Course descriptions (especially for high school)
- Samples of student work (optional but helpful)
- How Long: Through college admissions process

**Grades and Report Cards** (For High School):
- Semester or annual grades for each course
- Progress reports (optional, but helpful for transcript creation)
- How Long: Permanently (needed for college transcript)

**Notice of Intent Confirmation**:
- Copy of filed Notice of Intent to Operate Home School
- DNPE confirmation (if received)
- How Long: Permanently

### What You Do NOT Need to Keep

**Not Required**:
- Every assignment or homework paper
- Daily lesson plans
- Detailed daily records (unless you want them for personal use)
- Proof of curriculum approval (no approval needed)

## Homeschool Diploma

### Parent-Issued Diploma is Legal and Accepted

**NC Law**: Homeschool students receive diploma issued by parent (as school administrator)

**Colleges Accept Parent-Issued Diplomas**:
- All eight of your target universities accept homeschool diplomas
- No state-issued diploma available or needed in NC
- Parent-issued diploma has same legal standing as public school diploma

### How to Create a Homeschool Diploma

**Include**:
- School name (e.g., "[Your Homeschool Name]")
- Student's full legal name
- Statement: "Has successfully completed the requirements for high school graduation"
- Graduation date (month and year)
- Parent signature as "School Administrator" or "Director"
- Optional: School seal (you can create one)

**Format**:
- Can print on diploma paper (available at office supply stores or online)
- Can create digital design and print professionally
- Should look professional and official

**Example Text**:
```
GRIFFIN FAMILY HOMESCHOOL
Raleigh, North Carolina

This certifies that

CONNOR MICHAEL GRIFFIN

has successfully completed the requirements for
high school graduation and is awarded this

DIPLOMA

Given this 1st day of June, 2027

_____________________________________
[Parent Name]
School Administrator
```

## College Application Considerations

### What Universities Request from NC Homeschoolers

**Required Documents**:
- Official homeschool transcript (parent-created)
- Counselor recommendation (parent completes as counselor)
- Teacher recommendations (from non-parent instructors)
- Standardized test scores (if submitting - many schools are test-optional)

**NOT Required by Universities**:
- Proof of NC DNPE registration
- Annual standardized test scores (Iowa Tests, etc.)
- Attendance records
- Immunization records (not for admissions; required upon enrollment if admitted)
- Proof of compliance with NC homeschool law

**Universities Assume Compliance**:
- Colleges assume you are legally compliant with NC homeschool law
- They do not verify your DNPE registration
- Your homeschool is legitimate unless there's reason to believe otherwise

### How to Reference Your NC Homeschool Status

**On Applications**:
- School Name: "[Your Homeschool Name]" (or your registered school name)
- School Type: "Homeschool" or "Non-Public Home School"
- Counselor: Parent's name
- School CEEB Code: 000000 (homeschools use this code, or leave blank)

**In Counselor Letter** (parent writes):
- Briefly mention your homeschool is registered with NC DNPE
- Note compliance with NC homeschool law
- No need to provide detailed explanation of NC regulations

**Example**:
"[Student Name] has been homeschooled through [Your Homeschool Name], a registered non-public school with the North Carolina Division of Non-Public Education, since 9th grade. Our homeschool complies with all NC homeschool regulations, including annual standardized testing and curriculum requirements."

## Special Situations

### Transitioning from Public/Private School to Homeschool

**Steps**:
1. Officially withdraw from current school (in writing)
2. Request official transcript from previous school (keep for records)
3. File Notice of Intent with NC DNPE (within 30 days of starting homeschool)
4. Begin homeschool instruction
5. Continue documenting coursework and grades from point of transition

**Transcript Considerations**:
- Include courses from previous school on homeschool transcript
- Clearly note which courses were completed at previous school vs. homeschool
- Request official transcript from previous school to keep with homeschool records

### Transitioning from Homeschool to Public/Private School

**If the student decides to Return to Traditional School**:
1. Contact school to inquire about enrollment process
2. Provide homeschool transcript showing completed coursework and grades
3. School may require placement testing to determine grade level
4. Some homeschool credits may transfer; verify with school

**Planning Ahead**:
- Keep detailed records in case transition is needed
- Maintain standardized test scores showing academic progress
- Create professional transcript that traditional schools will recognize

### Moving to/from North Carolina

**Moving TO North Carolina**:
- File Notice of Intent within 30 days of establishing residency
- Continue homeschooling under NC law
- Bring records from previous state

**Moving FROM North Carolina**:
- Research homeschool laws in new state (they vary significantly)
- File Notice of Intent or equivalent in new state
- Bring all NC homeschool records with you

## Common Questions About NC Homeschool Law

### Do I need to be accredited to homeschool in NC?

**No**. Accreditation is NOT required. Parent-operated homeschools registered with DNPE are fully legal without any external accreditation.

### Can the state inspect my homeschool?

**Rarely**. DNPE does not conduct routine inspections of homeschools. Compliance reviews are uncommon and typically only occur if there are concerns raised. Maintaining records and complying with requirements (testing, attendance) is sufficient.

### What if my child doesn't test well on standardized tests?

**No penalty**. NC does not require minimum scores on annual standardized tests. Tests are to demonstrate progress, not to meet benchmarks. Low scores do not trigger state intervention in most cases.

### Can I use my own curriculum or do I need to follow a specific one?

**Your choice**. Parents choose curriculum and materials. No state approval needed. You can use published curriculum, create your own, use online courses, or mix approaches.

### Does my homeschool diploma have the same legal standing as a public school diploma?

**Yes**. Parent-issued homeschool diplomas in NC have the same legal standing as public school diplomas for purposes of college admission, employment, and military service.

### Can my homeschooled student participate in public school activities or sports?

**Varies by district**. Some NC school districts allow homeschool students to participate in extracurricular activities or sports. Contact your local school district to ask about policies.

### Can I homeschool through high school and still go to college?

**Absolutely**. Homeschool students are admitted to colleges and universities across the country, including all target schools in this guide. Colleges welcome homeschool applicants.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Homeschooling is Legal and Protected in NC**: Parents have the right to homeschool their children if they comply with state requirements.

2. **Registration is Simple**: File Notice of Intent with DNPE, maintain attendance records, and administer annual testing. That's it.

3. **No Curriculum Approval Needed**: Parents choose all curriculum and materials without state approval.

4. **Parent-Issued Diplomas Are Valid**: Your diploma has the same legal standing as public school diplomas for college admissions.

5. **Colleges Don't Verify NC Compliance**: Universities assume you're compliant; they don't request DNPE documentation.

6. **Keep Good Records**: Maintain attendance, testing, grades, and immunization records for your own protection and college applications.

7. **Annual Testing is for Progress, Not Pass/Fail**: Test scores do not need to be high; they demonstrate progress over time.

8. **High School Requirements Are Determined by Parents (and Colleges)**: NC doesn't mandate specific high school courses, but colleges expect college-prep curriculum.

Your compliance with NC homeschool law ensures your homeschool is legal and legitimate. Good record-keeping supports your college applications and provides documentation if ever needed. Homeschooling in NC is straightforward - register, teach, test annually, and document your work.
